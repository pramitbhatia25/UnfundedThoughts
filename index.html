The Itch to Startup

Written by a Founding Software Engineer at a YC Startup

I keep hearing people say they want to do a startup.

I say it too.

The sentence is easy. It takes two seconds. It sounds ambitious, almost responsible, especially now. AI is moving fast. Every week there is another company that seems to go from idea to billion-dollar headline before you even understand what they do.

But I do not think most people are lying. I think they are compressing a lot into one sentence.

  • Some people mean they want money.
  • Some people mean they want freedom.
  • Some people mean they want status.
  • Some people mean they are bored.
  • Some people mean they want to stop being one small part of somebody else's machine.
  • Some people mean they want a shot at becoming the kind of person they keep reading about.

The problem is that we compare the desire to the outliers.

We say startup and immediately think OpenAI, Stripe, Airbnb, Coinbase, DoorDash, Cursor. We skip the years where nobody cared. We skip the bad demos. We skip the 15-hour days and "code red" weekends. We skip the part where your product is embarrassing and your confidence depends on one customer replying to an email.

Maybe it is not really about startups.

Maybe it is about meaning. About needing a story big enough to organize your life around. About wanting proof that your work can turn into something more than a board full of tasks.

The founder stories we are fed give us that. A clean arc. A person starts with nothing, sees something others missed, suffers beautifully, and eventually becomes inevitable.

It is a good story.

A good story can make almost anything feel rational.

It can make exhaustion look like discipline. It can make sleep debt look like conviction. It can make ignoring the rest of your life look like focus.

Maybe that is why people keep reaching for it.

Not because they want to build a startup. Because they want to have built one.
