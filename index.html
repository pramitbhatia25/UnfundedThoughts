The Itch to Startup

Written by Pramit Bhatia

I keep hearing the same sentence.

I want to build a startup.

It comes up at dinner, in group chats, after someone sees another AI company raise a stupid amount of money, after a friend gets laid off, after a boring day at work where nothing moves except a calendar invite.

I get why the sentence is everywhere. AI made the first version of almost anything feel reachable. You can build a demo in a weekend. You can fake the team. You can make the landing page, the deck, the workflow, the assistant, the "wait this is actually kind of useful" prototype.

At the same time the feed is all outliers. Someone is twenty-four with a billion-dollar valuation. Someone shipped an ugly product and got acquired. Someone raised a seed round off a video. Someone is "building in public" and somehow the public part is already bigger than the building part.

It messes with your sense of probability. The winners are loud. The quiet majority just disappears. Nobody posts the screenshot of twelve months of nothing working.

The real question is not whether startups are cool. They are. The question is what the itch is actually asking for.

Sometimes it is ownership. Sometimes it is money. Sometimes it is boredom. Sometimes it is wanting proof that you are not just good at doing tasks inside someone else's machine.

And sometimes it is just status wearing a Patagonia vest.

The benefit, if there is one, is that a startup gives you direct feedback. Not polite feedback. Not manager feedback. Reality feedback. People use it or they do not. They pay or they do not. They come back or they forget you exist.

That is probably why the itch is so strong right now. Everything else feels indirect. The job market feels weird. The economy feels weird. AI makes building feel possible. The internet makes success feel nearby. So people reach for the sentence.

I want to build a startup.

Maybe. But I think the better first sentence is smaller and harder to fake: this problem keeps bothering me.
