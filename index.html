The Itch to Startup

Written by Pramit Bhatia

I hear some version of it all the time: I want to build a startup. Not a specific company. Not a customer. Just the startup. The word has become shorthand for escape: from a job, from slow promotions, from being one person inside someone else's plan.

The economy makes the feeling rational. Hiring feels weird, status feels portable, and the internet keeps showing people who turned a small product into a huge outcome. AI adds fuel because it collapses the distance between an idea and a prototype. You can open a laptop at midnight and produce something that used to require a small team.

That part is real. The cost of trying has fallen. The cost of convincing yourself you are building a company has fallen too.

The headlines make the probability feel different than it is. In Q1 2026, Crunchbase reported $300 billion in global startup funding, with 80% going into AI. Four companies - OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, and Waymo - took $188 billion by themselves. Carta saw the same shape from another angle: more than 60% of funding on its platform went to AI, while AI Series A valuations looked like a different market from non-AI.

So the market can look open and closed at the same time. Open because tools are cheaper. Closed because capital and attention concentrate around a few obvious stories.

Probability is the cold part. BLS data shows only about a third of one 2013 business cohort was still operating ten years later. Even in venture-backed seed startups, AngelList estimated roughly a 2.5% shot at becoming a unicorn. If the goal is only to become the screenshot in someone else's feed, the math is not kind.

But that does not mean the itch is stupid. It means the benefit has to be clearer than "maybe this works." The obvious benefits are autonomy, learning rate, ownership, and leverage. A startup gives you a game where effort can compound faster than a job, where the market replies quicker than a manager, and where your taste has consequences.

Those benefits can be real even if the company fails. You learn distribution, product judgment, selling, cash, discipline, and whether you actually like uncertainty or only like the aesthetics of it.

The honest test is simple: if no one could see you building it, would you still want to solve the problem? If the answer is no, maybe the itch was status. If the answer is yes, maybe there is something there.

Everyone wants to startup because the present economy makes employment feel less safe, AI makes creation feel more accessible, and the internet makes outlier success feel close enough to touch. The benefit is not that the odds become good. The benefit is that, for the right person and the right problem, the work becomes honest. You get direct contact with reality.

Maybe the first sentence should not be "I want to build a startup." Maybe it should be: "I cannot stop thinking about this problem, and I want to know if anyone else feels it too." That is less glamorous. It is also a better beginning.
